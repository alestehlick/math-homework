Instructions. Answer carefully in complete sentences when explanation is requested. The purpose of this homework is not only to remember vocabulary, but to use it correctly. When a question asks for a reason, a short but clear reason is enough.

Vocabulary to Use Carefully

  • p is sufficient for q means: if p is true, then q must be true.
  • q is necessary for p means: p cannot be true unless q is true.
  • p is necessary and sufficient for q means: p and q always have the same truth value.
  • p if and only if q means: p → q and q → p are both true.
  • Proof by contradiction for p → q: rule out the only bad case: p true and q false.
1. Translating a Conditional
Let p be “a number is divisible by 4,” and let q be “a number is even.” The statement p → q says:
If a number is divisible by 4, then the number is even.
Rewrite this same statement using the words sufficient and necessary.
  1. “Being divisible by 4 is __________________ for being even.”
  2. “Being even is __________________ for being divisible by 4.”

4. The Meaning of “Only If”
The phrase only if often causes mistakes. Consider the sentence:
A student may take the final exam only if the student has submitted the project.
Let p = “the student may take the final exam.” Let q = “the student has submitted the project.”
  1. Write the sentence as p → q or q → p.
  2. Which condition is necessary for taking the final exam?
  3. Does submitting the project automatically guarantee that the student may take the final exam? Explain.
5. When Is an “If and Only If” Statement Too Strong?
Consider the statement:
A number is divisible by 6 if and only if it is divisible by 3.
  1. Break the statement into two conditional statements.
  2. Decide which direction is true and which direction is false.
  3. Give a counterexample to the false direction.

8. Proof by Contradiction: An Everyday Example
We want to prove:
If a person is in Tokyo, then the person is in Japan.
A proof by contradiction rules out the case where the first part is true and the second part is false.
  1. What is the bad case that must be ruled out?
  2. Why is that case impossible?
  3. Write a short proof by contradiction in your own words.
9. Proof by Contradiction: Even and Odd
Prove the statement by contradiction:
If n is an even integer, then n is not odd.
Use the idea that a proof by contradiction rules out the case where p is true and q is false.
  1. What is p?
  2. What is q?
  3. What is the bad case?
  4. Explain why the bad case is impossible.
10. Proof by Contradiction: A Simple Inequality
Prove by contradiction:
If x > 5, then x > 3.
Do not just say “it is obvious.” Use the structure of contradiction.
  1. Assume the bad case: x > 5 and ____________________.
  2. Explain why these two statements cannot both be true.
  3. Write the proof in two or three sentences.
11. Contradiction and Counterexample
A student says:
“To prove that p → q is false, I should use proof by contradiction.”
Explain why this is not the usual method. What should the student find instead?
Hint: To show that p → q is false, you do not rule out the bad case. You exhibit it.
12. Final Synthesis
Consider the statement:
A triangle is equilateral if and only if all three of its angles are equal.
  1. Break the statement into two conditional statements.
  2. For each direction, say what is necessary and what is sufficient.
  3. Explain why this is a stronger claim than saying only: “If a triangle is equilateral, then all three of its angles are equal.”
  4. Suppose you wanted to prove one direction by contradiction. Choose one direction and state exactly what bad case you would rule out.
