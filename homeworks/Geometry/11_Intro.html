Reading: What Does Volume Mean?

When we say that a box has volume 24 cubic units, we mean something very concrete: if we filled the box with little cubes of size \(1\times1\times1\), exactly 24 of them would fit.

That idea—counting cubes—is the starting point. But geometry wants a definition that still makes sense for shapes that are not easy to fill directly with cubes (pyramids, cones, spheres). So we write down rules that any reasonable “amount of space” must satisfy. These rules are called postulates.

Postulate 1 — The unit cube sets the scale
Imagine measuring length without agreeing on what “1 unit” means—numbers would be meaningless. Volume works the same way.
Postulate: We define \(V(\text{unit cube})=1\).
Postulate 2 — Moving a solid does not change its volume
If you rotate a box, slide it, or flip it over, it does not suddenly contain more (or less) space.
Postulate: Same shape and size (even if moved) ⇒ same volume.
Postulate 3 — Volumes add when pieces do not overlap
If you cut a solid cleanly and keep all pieces, you did not destroy or create space. So the whole should equal the sum of its parts.
Postulate: If pieces do not overlap, then \(V(\text{whole})=V(\text{part 1})+\cdots\).
Why “no overlap” matters
If two pieces overlap, you would count the overlapping region twice. That would inflate the total and break the meaning of measurement.
Postulate 4 — Volume cannot be negative
You can have some space, or no space, but not “negative space.”
Postulate: Every solid has \(V\ge 0\).

A key consequence: if solid \(A\) fits inside solid \(B\), then \(A\) cannot have greater volume. Intuitively, \(B\) is “\(A\) plus leftover space,” and leftover space cannot be negative.

These postulates explain why volume formulas are not magic. For example, \(V=lwh\) for a box comes from packing it with unit cubes and adding (no overlap). They also justify cut-and-rearrange reasoning: if you cut a solid and rebuild it with no gaps and no overlaps, the volume stays the same.
